Eukaryotes & Prokaryotes (AQA GCSE Biology): Revision Note

Exam code: 8461
Written by: Lára Marie McIvor
Reviewed by: Lucy Kirkham
Updated on 31 March 2025

Cells in biology

All living organisms are made up of cells. Cells are the basic building blocks of life and can be classified into two main types: eukaryotic cells and prokaryotic cells. Understanding the differences between these cell types is fundamental to biology.

Eukaryotic cells are complex cells that contain a nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. Examples of eukaryotic organisms include animals, plants, fungi, and protists. Prokaryotic cells, on the other hand, are simpler cells that lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Bacteria and archaea are examples of prokaryotic organisms.

Eukaryotic cells

Eukaryotic cells are characterised by the presence of a membrane-bound nucleus that contains the cell's genetic material (DNA). They are typically larger and more complex than prokaryotic cells, ranging from 10-100 micrometres in diameter.

Key features of eukaryotic cells include:

  • A true nucleus enclosed by a nuclear membrane
  • Membrane-bound organelles such as mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum, and Golgi apparatus
  • Linear chromosomes made of DNA and proteins
  • Cell division by mitosis or meiosis

Prokaryotic cells

Prokaryotic cells are much simpler in structure compared to eukaryotic cells. They lack a true nucleus and their DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid. Prokaryotic cells are generally smaller, typically 0.1-5 micrometres in diameter.

Key features of prokaryotic cells include:

  • No true nucleus - DNA is found in the nucleoid region
  • No membrane-bound organelles
  • Circular chromosome (single loop of DNA)
  • May have plasmids (small rings of extra DNA)
  • Cell division by binary fission
