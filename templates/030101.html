Living & Non-Living Things (Primary 3 Science)

Lower Block Themes: Diversity · Cycles · Interactions
Written by: BackBenchAI Primary Science Team
Reviewed by: MOE-aligned Educator
Updated on 10 October 2025

What makes something living?

In Primary 3 Science, we learn to sort things into living and non-living. Living things share common characteristics: they need air, water and food, they grow, they can respond to changes, and they can reproduce (make more of their kind). Non-living things do not carry out all these life processes.

We use observable features to classify: for example, a cat grows and breathes (living), but a rock does not (non-living). Plants are living too — they make their own food and grow from seeds.

How we group animals

We classify animals into groups using observable features. For example, mammals usually have body hair and produce milk for their young; birds have feathers; fish have scales and fins; reptiles often have dry scales; amphibians have smooth, moist skin; insects have 6 legs and 3 body parts. Classification helps us describe and compare living things clearly.

How we group plants

Plants can be grouped as flowering or non-flowering. We also compare leaf shapes and veins, types of stems (woody or non-woody), and whether plants make seeds or spores. These features help us identify plants we see in gardens and parks.

Quick checks & common misconceptions

“It moves, so it must be living.” Not always. A toy car can move but it does not grow or reproduce — it is non-living.

“Plants don’t breathe.” Plants do take in air and release gases — they are living.

“Seeds are not living.” Seeds are living but in a resting state. With water, air and warmth, they can germinate into young plants.
